Philippine Payroll: Deductions & Take-Home Pay

Monthly breakdown and period totals for April–December 2026 and January–December 2027

Rules Applied (Philippine laws: NIRC, RA 10963 TRAIN Law, PD 851, RR 11-2018, RA 11199 SSS Law, RA 11223 UHC Act, HDMF Circular 460)
  • BIR withholding tax: TRAIN Law brackets, computed on (gross taxable compensation − mandatory employee contributions)
  • Special allowance: Fixed monthly cash allowances outside the de minimis list are fully taxable
  • 13th Month Pay (PD 851): = total basic salary earned during the year ÷ 12. Allowances are excluded. First ₱90,000 is tax-exempt (TRAIN Law); excess is taxable.
  • SSS: 15% total rate (5% EE + 10% ER + EC). MSC ceiling ₱35,000, includes allowances
  • PhilHealth: 5% premium (2.5% EE + 2.5% ER). Base = basic salary only, floor ₱10K, ceiling ₱100K
  • Pag-IBIG: 2% EE + 2% ER, capped at ₱10,000 monthly base (max ₱200 each)

13th Month Pay Computation

Under PD 851, the 13th month pay equals the total basic salary earned during the calendar year divided by 12. Allowances are not included. Under the TRAIN Law, the first ₱90,000 of 13th month + other benefits is tax-exempt.

Key Insight
On a monthly basis, Employee B takes home ₱400 more than Employee A because PhilHealth is computed only on basic salary (₱750 vs ₱1,250) — saving B ₱500/month, partially offset by ₱100 more in BIR tax. However, when the 13th month pay is included, Employee A pulls ahead annually — A's 13th month is ₱50,000 (full basic) while B's is only ₱30,000 because the ₱20,000 special allowance is excluded from 13th month pay computation. Net annual difference (2027): Employee A earns ₱15,200 more take-home than Employee B once 13th month is included, despite identical monthly gross. The employer also pays ₱26,000 more per year for Employee A due to the larger 13th month obligation.
